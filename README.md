# TraCe-bMPS

**The Translational Center for Barrier Microphysiological Systems**

TraCe-bMPS is a consortium of industry and academic partners developing and applying chip-based models of human tissue for both discovery research and regulatory drug development. We build microphysiological systems (MPS) that recreate the barriers that compartmentalize human tissue, enabling in situ measurements of tissue phenotypes and real-time observation of the interactions between cells and molecules in adjacent compartments.

Website: https://trace-bmps.org

## Mission

TraCe-bMPS creates human-relevant, physiologically accurate in vitro models of tissue barriers and the cellular crosstalk that drives health and disease. By pairing ultrathin silicon-nitride nanomembranes with a modular, rapidly-assembled chip architecture, we provide a platform that is accessible to non-engineering laboratories while still supporting sophisticated mechanistic and translational studies. Resource sharing and collaboration are a central part of the Center's mission.

## Goals

- Develop chip-based human tissue models that reveal disease mechanisms and accelerate identification of new therapeutics.
- Advance core technologies (ultrathin nanomembranes, modular device architectures, on-chip photonic sensors, and microfluidic integration) that bring tissue barriers into focus.
- Enable broad adoption of our platforms across academia and industry through open-access protocols, training, workshops, and a central resource core.
- Support both discovery and regulatory drug development use cases for the MPS community.
- Foster collaboration between academic labs, commercial partners, and the wider MPS community.

## Core Technologies

### µSiM (microphysiological system featuring a Silicon Membrane)
A modular tissue chip platform built around an ultrathin (< 100 nm) silicon-nitride nanomembrane with ~10^10 nanopores/cm². The membrane is thinner than the wavelength of visible light, giving glass-like imaging quality while offering no measurable diffusion resistance to small molecules. The µSiM uses a 9 mm × 18 mm multiwell-standard footprint with a 100 µL open apical well and a tapered microfluidic basal channel, assembled in minutes from mass-produced thermoplastic components and silicone pressure-sensitive adhesives (PSAs), with no microfabrication expertise required.

### hToC (human Tissue-on-a-Chip)
An extension of the µSiM that couples a vascular barrier to a 3D tissue compartment. The hToC features dual-scale nanomembranes (nanopores for diffusion plus patterned micropores for cell trafficking and direct cell-cell contact) and rigid tissue-support struts that anchor contractile hydrogels. Its two-component design allows the barrier and 3D tissue compartments to be cultured independently under their own optimal conditions, then combined non-destructively for integrated experiments or later disassembled for compartment-specific analysis.

### Modular architecture
Common design principles, including a shared footprint, pressure-sensitive adhesive assembly, and interchangeable membrane, top, and bottom components, turn every element into a swappable module. This supports nanoporous, dual-scale, or microporous membranes; deep chambers, hydrogel anchors, or microchannels; and add-on flow inserts, all without changing core handling and imaging workflows.

### Photonic sensors
Through a partnership with the Miller lab (University of Rochester) and AIM Photonics, TraCe integrates functionalized photonic biosensors directly adjacent to cells in chip-based MPS. This enables real-time, continuous, label-free quantification of secreted analytes (cytokines and other proteins) over the full length of an experiment, a capability not possible with endpoint assays alone.

## Projects

- **Blood-Brain Barrier (µSiM-BBB):** Human BBB models on the µSiM platform developed with the Engelhardt lab (University of Bern), including shear-conditioned, pericyte-enriched, and hiPSC-derived BMEC barriers for studies of inflammation, immune cell transmigration, and astrogliosis.
- **Tendon-on-a-Chip (hToC):** A vascularized fibrotic tendon model developed with the Awad, McGrath, and Miller labs (University of Rochester) for studying peritendinous fibrosis, the myofibroblast microenvironment, synovial adhesions, and therapeutic screening under flow.
- **Photonic sensor integration:** Real-time, continuous biosensing of cytokines and other secreted analytes on µSiM and hToC chips.

## Resources

TraCe-bMPS supports the community through the following resources:

- **TraCe Services:** a University of Rochester Service Center providing materials, methods, training, and design assets. Offerings include:
  - µSiM and hToC devices, components, accessories, and toolkits via the TraCe Chip Shop.
  - Hands-on training in device assembly, multicellular culture, shear/flow workflows, imaging, and functional assays.
  - Artwork libraries (BioRender and Adobe Illustrator) for schematics, workflows, and figures.
  - Validated, quality-system-aligned protocols and SOPs.
  - Expert consultation and troubleshooting (virtual or in-person).
  - Computational and online resources: calculators, design files, workflow guides, analysis tools, COMSOL resources.
  - Custom model development for tissue- and disease-specific applications.
- **Protocols:** open-access protocol library for µSiM and hToC workflows.
- **Cell Resources:** documented sourcing and differentiation protocols (e.g., pericyte differentiation, materials).
- **Video Gallery:** training and methods videos.
- **Publications:** peer-reviewed papers covering platform development, membrane science, barrier biology, and application models.
- **Blog:** updates on Center activities and research.
